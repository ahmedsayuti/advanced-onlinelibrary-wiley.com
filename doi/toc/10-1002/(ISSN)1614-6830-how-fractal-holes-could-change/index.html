How Fractal "Holes" Could Change the Way We Cool Our Devices

Date: 16th September 2024

When most people think about cooling hot electronics, they picture solid metal fins pulling heat away from a surface like the radiator in a car. Ayomide Ayoola's research turns that idea on its head by asking a simple but powerful question: what if cutting carefully arranged holes into those fins actually makes them better at their job?

Fig: Heating of Fractal Fins at (a) 90°

Fig: Heating of Fractal Fins at (b) 0°

At the heart of this work is a geometric pattern called the Sierpinski carpet—a repeating, self-similar grid where squares are removed again and again in a fractal fashion. Instead of using plain rectangular fins, Ayoola designs aluminum fins patterned with multiple "generations" of this carpet, then studies how well they shed heat when simply exposed to air, a process known as natural convection. In everyday terms, the research tests whether a smarter pattern of emptiness can beat traditional solid metal when it comes to keeping things cool.

One of the most striking findings is that these fractal fins become dramatically more effective when performance is measured per unit of weight. As the pattern grows more intricate from one fractal iteration to the next, the fin loses mass but gains a great deal of extra surface area, allowing air to flow through and around it more easily. Overall efficiency drops slightly because the heat has to travel through a more complex, porous structure, but when the same results are evaluated per gram of material, the higher iteration fins clearly win—especially when mounted vertically, where gravity naturally helps warm air rise and cool air replace it.

This blend of trade-offs is what makes the research compelling beyond theory. Modern technology, from electric vehicles to compact power electronics and aerospace systems, needs cooling solutions that are not only powerful but also light, compact, and passive, avoiding pumps or fans whenever possible. Ayoola's experiments show that fractal fins can satisfy all these demands at once: they move heat efficiently, weigh less than traditional designs, and rely only on the surrounding air to do their work. It is an elegant example of how a mathematical idea drawn from fractal geometry can be turned into a practical engineering tool for the next generation of high-performance, energy-efficient devices.
